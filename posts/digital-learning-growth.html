EdTech Growth in the Middle East & Africa

Published June 2025

The education technology landscape in the Middle East and Africa is transforming rapidly. Once viewed as nascent markets, both regions are now home to vibrant ecosystems of start‑ups, governments and investors working to reimagine how people learn at every stage of life. This article examines the factors driving growth, highlights key statistics and considers what the future may hold for learners and organisations alike.

Market Growth & Statistics

Analysts estimate that the e‑learning market in the Middle East and North Africa is growing at close to double‑digit rates each year. Surveys of employers and universities indicate that more than 70 percent of organisations in the region have already implemented some form of digital learning. Gamified courses report completion rates approaching 90 percent, while mobile learning has become the preferred access method for most learners as smartphone penetration rises.

Regional start‑ups are attracting attention as well. The Middle East & North Africa EdTech 50 ranking, for example, notes that roughly 40 percent of the cohort are based in the United Arab Emirates and Saudi Arabia, reflecting those countries’ ambitious national education strategies. Egypt’s dynamic innovation ecosystem contributes around one‑third of the top EdTech companies, underscoring its growing role in the African marketplace.

Drivers & Initiatives

Several forces are propelling this growth. Governments across the GCC and Africa have launched national visions and digital transformation initiatives that prioritise education and workforce development. Public‑private partnerships and venture funding have increased as leaders recognise the need for skills that match the demands of a digital economy. The COVID‑19 pandemic accelerated adoption of online learning platforms, prompting schools and companies to invest in robust infrastructure and professional development.

The regions’ youthful populations also play a role. With a median age well below the global average, the Middle East and Africa have millions of digital natives eager to engage with interactive, mobile and social learning. Demand for upskilling and reskilling among adults has likewise spurred the creation of flexible online programmes that fit busy schedules.

Implications for Digital Learning

The momentum in EdTech opens opportunities—and challenges—for educators, employers and learners. Institutions must ensure that digital courses are inclusive, accessible and culturally relevant. Translating and localising content into learners’ native languages significantly improves comprehension and engagement. Incorporating examples drawn from local contexts helps make abstract concepts concrete and relatable.

Artificial intelligence, adaptive learning and data analytics are enabling personalised learning pathways that adjust to each learner’s pace and preferences. When deployed responsibly, these tools can help reduce dropout rates and support the success of historically under‑served learners. At the same time, developers and policymakers must remain attentive to issues of data privacy, equity and the digital divide.

Conclusion

The Middle East and Africa stand at a promising juncture in the evolution of education. With supportive policies, growing investment and a new generation of innovators, the regions are poised to set examples for how digital learning can advance social and economic development. By continuing to invest in research, partnerships and evidence‑based practices, stakeholders can ensure that technology serves as a bridge rather than a barrier for learners everywhere.
